@Anonymous Elisa explains it in lecture 2. It is possible to construct an oracle for any function f(x) as a quantum gate by taking in |x,y> and outputting |x,y⊕f(x)>. One drawback of constructing an oracle this way is that the ancilla qubit |y> (ancilla because it is not part of the input for f) is affected by the operation. @Anonymous the reason you see a different matrix is because qiskit orders its qubits slightly differently. When you talk of the state |ab>, you generally mean the first qubit is in state |a> and the second qubit is in state |b>. But in qiskit notation it's reversed - because qiskit considers the first qubit as the least significant and the last qubit as the most significant one. What this implies for a 2 qubit system - when you talk of the state |01> , qiskit is thinking of it as the state |10>. And when you talk of the state |10>, qiskit treats it like the state |01>. Notice that for |00> and |11> it doesn't matter which way you consider them because they're the same anyway... What this implies for the CNOT matrix - the matrix in your first picture follows the qiskit ordering of qubits. Take a look at the columns. The first column is what you would get if you applied CNOT to |00>. You get (1 0 0 0) which is |00> - all good. Now look at the second column, which would normally tell you the effect of the matrix on |01>. According to the matrix you get (0 0 0 1) which corresponds to |11> - this doesn't seem right. But remember that qiskit thinks of your |01> as |10> and so it gives you |11>. Similarly on the third column you would expect to see the output when applied to |10>. Qiskit sees this as |01> and since |01> is unchanged by the CNOT gate qiskit gives you back |01>. But what qiskit sees as |01> is actually |10> and so you get the output (0 0 1 0). This can get confusing at first, but you'll get used to it. A good rule of thumb for 2x2 matrices is to take the matrix qiskit gives you and swap rows 2 and 3, and then swap columns 2 and 3. This way you're nullifying the effect of the qiskit ordering. Do this to your first matrix and you'll see it matches your second matrix (the one we know as the CNOT)

@Anonymous Quick answers since it's late, other mentors can perhaps elaborate later: 1. The transmon Hamiltonian has a sinusoidal term, which to first approximation (keeping the first term in the taylor expansion) is a duffing oscillator. I.e. the Duffing oscillator is an approximate model for the transmon Hamiltonian. 2. Typically, an IBM transmon is designed to work at a single, fixed frequency. 4-8 GHz is a band of microwave frequencies that's convenient to work in. Several design architectures are possible, but one common approach is to design the transmon's fixed frequency to be somewhere in the lower half of this frequency band (roughly), with the transmon's readout resonators in the upper half of the band (also roughly). Other variants of transmons also exist that can be tuned in frequency in-situ via a magnetic field applied through a superconducting loop (SQUID) built into the device. 5. Measurement is a physical process; it takes time for the measurement field in the readout resonator to glean enough information about the state of the qubit to fully resolve it's state. Moreover, a lot of that information acquired by the field on-chip is lost as the field travels from the qubit chip, through microwave components and amplifiers, and out to room temperature detectors, and this partial loss of information can make the overall process take even longer. It's possible to speed up the process a little using stronger measurement pulses, but at large enough fields undesirable nonlinear effects come into play that can destroy the qubit state, or cause other problems, limiting how far this can be pushed.

@Anonymous Quantum mechanics is a set of universal rules that can in principle be applied to anything. In many every-day systems, we don't see its effects because we are typically living on scales many orders of magnitude larger than the discrete quanta involved; since we can't resolve the quanta, we just see continuous behavior. @Anonymous @Anonymous Qiskit Aer has multiple simulator backends. One of which is multiplying matrices, but not all of them - for example if you wanted to debug a quantum circuit by stepping through the statevector after each gate, that isn't possible efficiently for some of the simulator backends. Both on simulator and on hardware, there is a basis set of gates used. A gate set that can implement any arbitrary unitary is called a universal gate set, of which the most popular is the Clifford + T gate set where the Clifford gates are classically simulatable efficiently. Therefore, in fault-tolerant quantum computing (aka a future era in which quantum error correction is below the threshold necessary to correct errors faster than they accumulate), the complexity of a quantum algorithm is on the order of the number of T gates. Qiskit has transpilers which compile from the gates you provide it to the gates you want to ultimately run. In classical computing, compilers are the code that translates a human-understandable programming language into something the computer can understand better (for example assembly code, which is 0's and 1's). The reason Qiskit calls them transpilers is because unlike a compiler, the starting and ending gates are still the same level of abstraction - still gates, just different gate sets. On IBM Q's hardware, after Qiskit transpiles your gates to a certain gateset - you can view the gateset decomposition of your gates with circuit.decompose() - those gates are then converted into microwave pulses. There are some optimizations and promising error correction techniques that can be further done at the pulse level, and we got a taste of Pulse in labs.

@Anonymous Nandigam i don't know of any open-source learning resources that give overviews of many different types of qubits, but we are actively working on that in the discord server that sprouted from the quantum-education study group channel here - link to join in the quantum-education channel. in theory many two-level quantum systems can be a qubit, where there's two quantum states that can be in superposition. in reality there are a lot of considerations as to what makes a good qubit: if your information bleeds off to other states outside those 2 states, if you can reliably address operations on the qubits ex. to entangle or measure them, how suited/expensive existing technology is that is necessary to do operations on your qubits, etc. There's an important tradeoff between how isolated a qubit is from its environment to reduce environmental noise, and how easily you can do operations - this is because in order to do operations on your qubit you have to send signals to it from its outside environment.
