Anonymous 19-Jul-20 11:52 PM
It is an ab initio approach, correct. Coupled-cluster theory uses an exponential cluster operator acting on Hartree-Fock wave-function to create a multi-electron wave-function to account for electron correlation. Wave-function based methods are systematically improbable and are preferred ( compared with DFT) for several problems in chemistry. Although this higher accuracy comes at increased cost of computation.
Coupled-cluster method with singles doubles and perturbative triples (CCSD(T)) is often referred as ‚Äúgold standard‚Äù in computational chemistry.

@Anonymous let's say u have 2 fermions (specifically, electrons in today's case): you wouldn't be able to represent it in the second state. the second state describes the state of ONE qubit at a time. similarly, the first one (with 4 digits) represents the states of FOUR qubits at a time. like Antonio pointed out in today's lecture, the mapping b/w fermion modes (which can be occupied by fermions-electrons, in today's case) and the qubits needed to represent all those modes is 1-1 (it can be a direct one, like in the JW mapping, or a more complicated one, like in the BK mapping). so, u need to use AT LEAST a 2-bit representation. the former (4-bit) state has more qubits than our minimum requirements, but that is okay (those extra bits will help build the "unoccupied" fermion modes/orbitals): in fact, we need the extra qubits for the UCCSD method he mentioned in the lecture later on to get a better/lower energy (the operator we need to build for this method only acts on our state, describing 2 fermions, if we have more fermionic modes than we have fermions: so we need to have more than the bare minimum 2 fermionic modes/qubits, otherwise it'll be an identity operator). does this make sense?
